01 — Historical Context

Where the Story Begins

She did not choose to cross the border. The border crossed her — or rather, war crossed her path before she was old enough to speak its name. Born in the late nineteenth century in the Tagbana region of what is now northern Côte d'Ivoire, our great-great-grandmother entered a world already fracturing under the pressure of colonial expansion and the devastating campaigns of Samory Touré.

At the time of her displacement, the region was caught between Touré's scorched-earth resistance and French colonial forces advancing inland. Entire villages were destroyed. People fled. Children were taken. Some were absorbed into new communities across the border into the Gold Coast — the territory that would one day become Ghana.

"She arrived not as a traveler — but as a child carried by the current of someone else's war."

She was brought to Senase, in the Berekum area of the Brong-Ahafo region. She was a toddler. No one alive today knows the exact name of the village she came from. The family later traced the region to Tagbana — a name that has held, however loosely, across four generations of oral memory.

02 — First Person Narrative

In Her Own Words

Narrator

Ama Dukuu's Story

As told through the memory of her descendants — reconstructed from oral testimony, Feb 2026

I do not remember my mother's face. I do not remember the sound of her voice, or the smell of the place where I was born. What I know of myself — what I have always known — is this: I arrived in Senase as a stranger. Someone carried me. I do not know who. I do not know why they chose me.

They gave me to a family. I grew up in their compound. I drank their water, I ate their food, I learned their words. When I was old enough to understand the shape of the question, I asked where I had come from. They said: across the border. They said: there was war. They said: you were very small.

I was never allowed to forget that I was not from here. It was not always said with cruelty. Sometimes it was just the way people looked at me — a pause before they used my name, a slight pull at the corner of the eye. It is a strange thing to be foreign in the only home you have ever known.

"I built my house with my own hands. They watched and said nothing. But the walls stood. And the walls remembered."

I married. I had children — four of them. Two of them went back, taken by illness or by fate, in the way that children were taken in those days. One son ran when the fighting spread — they said a stone caught him in the crowd. The last remained.

When they destroyed what I had, I rebuilt. When they told me again that I did not belong, I put my hands in the earth and I stayed. I planted. I built. I raised what I had left. That, too, is an answer.

By the time I was old, the land knew my name. My children's children spoke it. The trees I planted had grown roots deeper than anyone could cut. I came from Côte d'Ivoire — or so they say. But I belong to this ground. I am made of it now.

03 — Primary Research

The Interview

Conducted by Kyeremeh Alfred & Ibrahim Mahmoud · February 25, 2026 · Senase, Berekum

Q — 01
Where did our great-great-grandmother come from originally?
A — 01
She is believed to have come from Tagbana, in northern Côte d'Ivoire — though the exact village is not known. The family is not entirely certain even of the region.
Q — 02
How did she come to be in Senase, Berekum?
A — 02
She was brought over as a toddler — carried across during a period of conflict. Someone brought her; it is not known who or exactly under what circumstances.
Q — 03
Was she accepted in the community where she settled?
A — 03
Not fully. She was always known as a foreigner — the word was used, not always harshly, but consistently. She was an outsider even after decades of living there.
Q — 04
What challenges did she face as a woman building her life there?
A — 04
She lost two children. One son was killed in communal violence. Her possessions were destroyed during an outbreak of hostility. She was forced to rebuild, largely alone.
Q — 05
How did she respond to being displaced and marginalized?
A — 05
She built a house. Literally — with her own hands. She stayed. She raised what she had. By staying, she eventually earned a kind of belonging that was never officially given to her.
Q — 06
What is the family's connection to her today?
A — 06
She is the root of the entire family as they know it in Senase. Everything — the names, the land, the community standing — traces back to her survival and to the life she built against resistance.

04 — Written Reflection

What the Story Taught Me

The story of our great-great-grandmother is one that resonates deeply with our current community. It teaches us how communities were formed in the past, and, more specifically, it tells us how we, as a family, became settlers in our present destination.

In the act of sculpting the story as we heard it from our grandmother, we decided firsthand to take only into consideration the part of her story that relates to her and ignore any other parts that might be about someone else. This required us to be a bit biased by also excluding any relationship that existed with the persona (great-great-grandmother) and someone else, but she wasn't the main idea in that story.

Additionally, with the exception of the integration of stories from our secondary research that aligns with the story of our great-great grandmother, we also included more of the part of the story from our primary resource which was more about her. Although we cannot determine the bias that existed when we retrieved the story firsthand from our grandmother, we decided to make very few changes from the story that we received.

We did not have to translate the interview verbatim as we heard it, because there would be a lot of challenges in that. However, since it was a story that we had heard before, we decided to focus more on the bullet points and expatiate on the part of the story that really concerns the persona of the story. For the sake of creative writing, we endeavored to also consider certain part of the main story which really did not concern her so that we can decide if we can make connections between her and that story to make it creative.

During the interview with our grandmother, she was more interested in sharing about the personal life of the persona. However, when it got to the point where she was to talk about where exactly the persona is from, she became a bit uncomfortable. She said that where the persona is actually from is unknown, and even the assumed place, which is "Tagbana," might not be the real place where she is from. Nonetheless, Tagbana was assumed to be the place where our great-great grandmother might have been taken from because the children in the family, on their pursuit of finding our root found a woman who looked exactly like our great grandmother. But according to traditions, it is assumed that it is not right to look for where we might be from after years of it being unknown.

"From this story, I realized how some identities are not built based on personal consciousness but are given as a decision made by nature."

During the interview, we were more sensitive to the emotions of our grandmother. This resonated from our emotionally sensitive story is, and the memories that it brings, even when the persona's name is mentioned. This brought a matter of concern to consider such moments and allow her to speak up without stopping her; however, at situations where we thought we would not need more information, we asked a different question to change the trajectory.

The same idea was integrated into our writing. We endeavored to emphasize more on the parts of the story that really resonated with her, and tried to expand more on that by giving more details.

We chose to structure the story in a way that is more sensitive because it is relevant to the story gotten from our interviewee. Nevertheless, we endeavored to maintain the voice of the interviewee in all of our story writing by making very few changes to suit the format of the story we wanted to tell.

From this assignment, we recognize the role of oral tradition as the very custodian of history, with ours not being an exception. Although I believe there might be a possibility that some parts of the story are not fully known yet, the main idea remains the same from generation to generation.

Some advice we would give to anyone embarking on this work is to make complete use of their creative skills. Additionally, they should not be deceived to completely alter the story they receive from their interviewee, but should endeavor to firsthand understand the point of view of the interviewee before making any changes later.

05 — Creative Work

The Poem

"She Was Once Called Foreign"

She does not remember Côte d'Ivoire's shore,

No lullabies, no mother's door.

Only dust that rose beneath fleeing feet,

And small bare steps on foreign street.

Too young to know what loss could mean,

Too young to grasp what she had seen,

She drank from arms not hers by birth,

Borrowed milk on borrowed earth.

She grew where strangers spoke her name,

Where soil and sorrow felt the same.

She learned the language of that land,

With trembling heart and steady hand.

They called her foreign. Soft but clear.
A whispered word she learned to hear.
Though she had nowhere else to claim,
"Outsider" still became her name.

She married under Berekum skies,

Built her hope where memory dies.

Four children cradled at her side —

Two returned where ancestors hide.

One son ran where the people fled,

Where fear like wildfire quickly spread.

A single stone through chaos thrown

Turned breath to silence, flesh to stone.

Her pots, her cloth, her chickens tied,

Cast to the storm and pushed aside.

The message clear in thunder's roar:

"You do not belong here anymore."

The rain fell hard, but still she stood.

Roots grow deepest where they could.

For though the land denied her claim,

Her sweat and blood had marked the same.

And then — one hand reached through the night,

Not armed with stone, but holding light.

A place to rest. A door made wide.

A quiet space where hope could hide.

From broken ground she chose to build,

With patient hands the storms had stilled.

Brick by brick, her house arose,

Against the doubt the village chose.

They called her foreign — once again.
But walls remember more than men.
For every nail and wooden beam
Declared her right to stay and dream.

Years unfolded. Children grew.

Roots pushed deep and branches knew

The land that once refused her tread

Now held her lineage widespread.

She was once carried, small and torn,

Across a line by conflict drawn.

But now her name the earth repeats:

In family homes and village streets.

They called her foreign. Let them speak.
The strong are not the ones who seek
Permission from a shifting land —
They build their place with steady hand.

What began in loss and flight

Now stands in brick and morning light.

The child once taken, far from shore,

Is foreign to this land no more.

06 — Research & Evidence

Historical Context

I. Historical Conflicts in Côte d'Ivoire & the Era of Slave Raiding

The displacement of our great-great-grandmother did not happen by accident. The late nineteenth century was an extremely difficult time for communities living along the border between Côte d'Ivoire and Ghana. A powerful military leader named Samory Touré built a large empire across what is now Guinea, northern Côte d'Ivoire, Mali and Sierra Leone. From the 1880s onward, as French colonial forces pushed further inland, Samory moved his empire eastward and used destructive methods — burning villages and taking people by force — to slow the French advance. His campaigns brought immense suffering to communities across northern Côte d'Ivoire and forced thousands to flee their homes. Children were among the most vulnerable during this period.

Slave raiding was also a major part of this regional history. Records show that the slave trade caused serious conflict between different ethnic groups, and communities that were weaker were often forced to flee or be absorbed by stronger ones. The capture of young children during these conflicts was not unusual and has been documented in both oral and written histories across West Africa.

The Berekum area of Ghana — where our great-great-grandmother was brought — sits in the Brong-Ahafo region, close to the Côte d'Ivoire border. Her experience of being taken as a toddler and brought across what are now national borders reflects a pattern of forced displacement that was widespread in West Africa during this period.

II. Displacement and Belonging in West Africa

Researcher Jean Pierre Misago has identified a situation he calls "permanent temporality" — describing how long-term migrants in Africa are often treated as temporary visitors by the communities they live in, no matter how many years they have stayed. Our great-great-grandmother lived this reality. She had no homeland to return to, and yet the community around her still treated her as an outsider.

Research published in Frontiers found that although displacement causes serious problems for a person's sense of identity and belonging, it also produces strong resilience and the ability to build new communities and networks. The fact that our great-great-grandmother built her own house and raised her children independently is a strong example of this kind of resilience. She did not only build a physical structure. She built a claim to the place she had been denied.

Scholar Loren Landau has argued that human movement within Africa changes the meaning of concepts like home, community and belonging, and that over time people who migrate create new forms of membership in the communities they move to. Belonging was not given to our great-great-grandmother. She built it through her presence, her endurance, and her contribution to that community over many years.
